Product Market and Retail Information

Background Information
The thermometer was invented in 1612 when Santorio Santorio applied a scale to an air thermoscope, thus creating an air thermometer. A thermoscope was capable of distinguishing between different temperatures, but without a scale of any kind. The first modern thermometer was invented in 1714 by Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit in 1714. Fahrenheit was a German physicist who introduced the standard temperature scale that bears his name. He divided the freezing and boiling points of water into 180 degrees; 32 degress was the freezing point and 212 degrees was the boiling point. This scale was based around the temperature of the human body at 100 degrees, which has since been adjusted to 98.6 degrees.

More on Expert Grill Thermometer
This pocket thermometer was developed by a company called Expert Grill, which is a brand developed by and for Walmart. All Expert Grill products’ manufacturing is outsourced by Walmart to a company called NexGrill, which is also responsible for manufacturing grills for Costco, Home Depot, and Lowes.

Reassembly



Lessons Learned
Digging into the history of a product and breaking into it to see its inner workings has been a super fascinating assignment. However, this task has come with many challenges that took unique problem-solving and critical thinking to overcome. When trying to reverse engineer products that are on the market, you quickly find out that a lot of information is proprietary and not easy to find. At first, I imagined that the schematics for the product would be readily available on the internet so this assignment wouldn't be very difficult, but boy was I wrong! It took countless hours of using a flashlight, magnifying glass, and multimeter to pinpoint what was going on with some of the connections on the PCB. Another difficult part of this task was finding what some of the products were on the board that weren't labeled. I ended up having to take some educated guesses, specifically with the microcontroller which was covered in epoxy during production so it was impossible to tell what it was without destroying the product. All in all, this has been a wonderful experience and a great lesson in the world of Embedded Systems.
